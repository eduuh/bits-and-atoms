Photos, documents, code, media — terabytes that can't come back if they go. The whole homelab is allowed to fail. The data isn't.

## The 3-2-1 rule

Three copies, on two different media types, with one of them offsite. That's the rule in one line. Everything below is just an instance of it.

## My implementation

The live data sits on my PC's NVMe drive and on the Proxmox VMs. That's copy one.

Copy two is a [TrueNAS Scale](https://www.truenas.com/truenas-scale/) box running ZFS. Snapshots fire every 15 minutes, so a file I deleted at lunch is still there at dinner. The pool is RAID-Z2, which means two drives can die before I lose anything.

Copy three lives away from the house. Critical data (anything I'd cry about) is encrypted with [Restic](https://restic.net/) and pushed to [Backblaze B2](https://www.backblaze.com/cloud-storage) nightly. Media goes to a NAS at a friend's place; we swapped backup space and pretend that counts as offsite redundancy. It does.

## Testing restores

A backup is only as good as the restore. Monthly fire drill: pick a random file, pick a random VM, recover it. If the recovery is awkward, fix the awkward part before I need it for real.

I learned this one the hard way. The first time I needed a file out of B2 I'd never tested the restore path, and the password manager I needed to unlock the encryption key was on the laptop I was trying to restore.
