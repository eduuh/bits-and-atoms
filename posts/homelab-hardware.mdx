Before getting into software, the hardware. My homelab is three mini PCs. I picked them for the same reason most people do: small, quiet, cheap, and they sip power.

## Firewall and control plane: Glovary

![Glovary back panel with 4 ethernet ports](/images/Screenshot%202026-01-13%20at%2023.59.48.png)

![Glovary front panel](/images/Screenshot%202026-01-14%20at%2000.00.18.png)

This Glovary box does two jobs. It has four 2.5GbE Intel i226-V NICs, which is exactly what OPNsense wants. On the same machine, Proxmox runs a VM that acts as the Kubernetes control plane (`intel-c-firewall`).

**Specs:**
- CPU: Intel N100 (4 cores)
- RAM: 8 GB
- Storage: 128 GB NVMe
- Network: 4x 2.5GbE Intel i226-V
- Cost: 42,000 KSH

The N100 keeps surprising me. Firewall duties plus the Kubernetes API, and it isn't even warm.

## Primary worker: AMD mini PC

![AMD mini PC](/images/Screenshot%202026-01-14%20at%2001.20.23.png)

![Front panel ports](/images/Screenshot%202026-01-14%20at%2001.19.52.png)

![Back panel ports](/images/Screenshot%202026-01-14%20at%2001.20.01.png)

This AMD mini PC (`amd-w-minipc`) does most of the actual work. Sixteen cores, and most of my apps run on it.

**Specs:**
- CPU: AMD Ryzen (16 cores)
- RAM: 13 GB
- Storage: ~1 TB
- Network: 2.5GbE
- Cost: 40,000 KSH

The 2.5GbE port matters here. Storage chatter between nodes adds up fast.

## Storage worker: ACEMAGIC

![ACEMAGIC mini PC with display](/images/Screenshot%202026-01-14%20at%2001.18.46.png)

The ACEMAGIC (`intel-w-acemagic`) has a tiny display on the front showing power draw and temps. It's a small thing and I love it. This node handles the storage-heavy workloads.

**Specs:**
- CPU: Intel (4 cores)
- RAM: 16 GB
- Storage: ~1 TB
- Network: 2.5GbE
- Cost: 25,000 KSH

More RAM helps Longhorn with caching and storage work.

## Why mini PCs

Power. The whole cluster idles around 50W. Less than one regular server, and the electricity bill barely flinches.

Quiet. These live in my kitchen. Silence isn't optional.

Size. Three of them take up less space than a single rack-mount machine would.

Price. The whole cluster came to about 174,000 KSH. Try that with enterprise gear.

## Network

All three nodes plug into a 5-port 2.5GbE unmanaged switch. The Glovary handles routing and internet. For WiFi I'm using two Google Nest WiFi Pro units in bridge mode, also on the switch.

```
Internet → Glovary (OPNsense) → Managed Switch → Worker Nodes
                ↓
         Proxmox VM (Control Plane)
```

## Power use

I checked with a smart plug. Idle sits around 50W. Under load it's 80 to 100W.

That works out to about 36 kWh a month at idle. At 26 KSH per unit in Kenya, that's roughly 936 KSH a month to keep the whole cluster on, all the time.

For about 174,000 KSH I have a working Kubernetes cluster with distributed storage, running all day every day. The whole software stack (Talos, Kubernetes, Flux, Longhorn, Prometheus, all the self-hosted apps) is open source. None of it costs me anything.

One thing I learned: shipping from overseas was cheaper than buying locally. You wait a bit longer. You save a lot.

## What I'd change

A few things I'd do differently if I started today.

More RAM on the control plane. 8 GB works, but 16 GB would breathe easier.

Matching NICs across all three nodes. Mixed speeds make networking annoying for no good reason.

NVMe everywhere. A couple of nodes still have SATA SSDs. Etcd and databases notice.

## What's next

There's an old Dell laptop with an 8th-gen Intel i7 sitting on my desk. It's perfect for Jellyfin transcoding. The plan is to add it to the cluster and find a new role for the ACEMAGIC.

![Dell laptop internals ready for homelab duty](/images/20260114_192856.jpg)

## Room to grow

Every node has an empty SSD slot. The AMD mini PC, the Dell laptop, and the Glovary each have room for a 4TB drive. That's up to 12TB more storage without buying another machine.

The catch is price. 4TB 2.5" SSDs aren't cheap. For now the two Samsung 980 NVMe drives are handling things fine.

The plan is to max out the existing nodes before I think about a dedicated NAS. 12TB should keep me happy for a while.
