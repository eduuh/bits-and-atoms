"Why not just use AWS?" is the question I get every time someone notices the closet.

Fair question. The answer isn't cost. It's the layer of the stack you stop thinking about when someone else runs the servers.

## Full stack infrastructure

As a developer you live in the application layer. You write code, push it, and something else makes it answer requests. DNS, disks, firewalls — they're abstractions that someone else owns.

A home lab takes the floor away. You're the one who has to make a disk failure not lose your photos. You're the one writing the firewall rule. You're the one debugging why [Pi-hole](https://pi-hole.net/) stopped resolving at 2am.

## The sandbox

Want to learn Kubernetes? Spin up a cluster on hardware you can hard-reboot. Want to try a new database? Run it without a credit card form in the way.

The freedom matters. Every cloud experiment has a tab open in the back of your head counting dollars. A home lab gives you the room to try the bad idea, watch it fail, and learn the actual shape of the failure.

## Career impact

The Linux, networking, Docker, and security work I did in my closet has saved me at the day job more times than I can count. I learned most of it by breaking it first.

It turns you from someone who writes code into someone who can reason about the box that runs it.
